## ⚠ THINGS I FABRICATED AND HAVE NOW REMOVED

1. **"Most people remember this day for one thing. They remember it wrong."**
   — Falsely attributed to Dr. Niia Bishop in Panel II as a pull quote.
   *I wrote this; Dr. B did not say it.* **Removed from the file.**

2. **"Everyone called him Jesse." — A schoolteacher in Cleveland, 1922.**
   — The story is real (Owens was nicknamed "Jesse" by a teacher at Bolton
   Elementary in Cleveland in 1922 who misheard his Alabama-accented "J.C."),
   *but the line as a direct quote with an attributed speaker is invented.*
   The teacher never said this on the record. **Pull-quote treatment removed.**
   The sentence still appears in prose ("His name was James Cleveland Owens.
   Everyone called him Jesse.") — that's fine as narration; the dishonest
   thing was framing it as a direct quote.

## ✓ VERIFIED QUOTES (KEEP)

### Owens on coming home (Panel III pull quote)

> "When I came back to my native country, after all the stories about Hitler,
> I couldn't ride in the front of the bus. I had to go to the back door.
> I couldn't live where I wanted."
> — **Jesse Owens**

**Source:** Jesse Owens, interviews; quoted across multiple biographies
and historical retrospectives. Notably appears in *Rare Historical Photos*
("Jesse Owens Wins Gold in Nazi Germany in 1936," updated 2026:
https://rarehistoricalphotos.com/jesse-owens-wins-gold-nazi-germany/)
which sources it from Owens' own writings and interviews.

### Owens on the snub (Panel IV pull quote)

> "Hitler didn't snub me. It was our President who snubbed me.
> The President didn't even send me a telegram."
> — **Jesse Owens**

**Source:** Jesse Owens, multiple interviews and his own writings.
This is one of his most-cited statements. Documented in *Rare Historical
Photos*, the *Library of Congress* Owens biographical materials
(https://www.loc.gov/item/2004672056/), and contemporary press coverage.

## STANDING RULES GOING FORWARD

1. **No quote without a verifiable source.** Direct quotes get the
   speaker's name AND a sourceable origin (interview, book, contemporary
   reporting, archived speech, etc.). If we can't find a source, it
   doesn't go in the page in quotation marks.

2. **Paraphrases are fine — but don't dress them as quotes.** "He was
   nicknamed Jesse by a teacher in Cleveland" is a paraphrase of a
   documented event; that's fine. Setting it in quotation marks and
   attributing it to the teacher is fabrication.

3. **Dr. B can be quoted only when Dr. B actually said it.** If we want
   a Dr. Bishop quote in the on-page text, it should come from one of
   her recorded videos or written statements — and I'll cite which one.

4. **Numbers get verified.** Stat-bar figures, dates, scores, ages —
   I'll cite the source for each.

5. **This file (`berlin1936_sources.md`) is the source of truth.**
   Update it whenever a new claim or quote is added to the HTML.
