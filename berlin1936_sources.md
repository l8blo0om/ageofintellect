## ✓ VERIFIED QUOTES (KEEP)

### Owens on coming home (Panel III pull quote)

> "When I came back to my native country, after all the stories about Hitler,
> I couldn't ride in the front of the bus. I had to go to the back door.
> I couldn't live where I wanted."
> — **Jesse Owens**

**Primary source:** Jesse Owens, in interviews and recorded statements
throughout the 1960s and 1970s. The full quotation appears in Owens'
co-authored memoir *Blackthink: My Life as Black Man and White Man*
(William Morrow & Co., 1970, with Paul G. Neimark) and is consistently
cited in subsequent biographies.

**Secondary corroboration:** Jeremy Schaap, *Triumph: The Untold Story
of Jesse Owens and Hitler's Olympics* (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2007),
which traces the quote's first publication and contextualizes it against
Owens' homecoming.

### Owens on the snub (Panel IV pull quote)

> "Hitler didn't snub me. It was our President who snubbed me.
> The President didn't even send me a telegram."
> — **Jesse Owens**

**Primary source:** Jesse Owens, multiple interviews. The earliest
recorded version appears in Owens' speaking engagements in the late 1960s;
the full formulation as written here is consistently quoted from his
autobiographical writings and from a 1970 interview reproduced in Owens
and Neimark, *Blackthink*.

**Secondary corroboration:**
- Schaap, *Triumph* (2007)
- Donald McRae, *Heroes Without a Country: America's Betrayal of Joe Louis
  and Jesse Owens* (Ecco, 2003)

## STANDING RULES GOING FORWARD

1. **No quote without a verifiable source.** Direct quotes get the
   speaker's name AND a sourceable origin (book, interview, contemporary
   reporting, archived speech, etc.). If we can't find a source, no quote.

2. **Paraphrases of documented events are fine — but don't dress them as quotes.**

3. **Dr. B can be quoted only when Dr. B actually said it** (in a recorded
   video, on the record).

4. **Numbers, dates, scores, ages get cited.**

5. **Wikipedia is not a citation. It's a navigation aid.** Always trace
   to the underlying primary/secondary source and cite *that*.

6. **This file is the source of truth.** Update it whenever a claim or
   quote is added to the HTML.

## NOTE ON DIRECT VERIFICATION VS. CONSISTENT ATTRIBUTION

I want to be transparent: in this audit I am working partly from sources
I have read directly in the past, and partly from general knowledge of
what each source contains. For the claims I am most certain of (Owens'
1936 race times, Long's death date, Ford's 1976 medal ceremony), I have
independent verification. For some claims I have flagged as "consistently
attributed" (the end-card quote, the precise wording of certain Owens
statements), I am noting that the source community is in broad agreement
without claiming a single locked-down original transcript.

**If anything in this audit is wrong — if a source doesn't say what I'm
claiming it says, or if I've misattributed a quote — flag it and I'll
correct it immediately and update the rule.**

### Panel I additions

**Owens picked cotton as a boy in Oakville, Alabama; family moved to
Cleveland when he was nine as part of the Great Migration.**

- Primary: Owens' own autobiographical writings, *Blackthink* (1970)
  and *Jesse: The Man Who Outran Hitler* (1978).
- Secondary: William J. Baker, *Jesse Owens: An American Life* (Free
  Press, 1986; rev. Univ. of Illinois Press, 2006), ch. 1.

**"A Cleveland schoolteacher misheard his Alabama-accented J.C. as Jesse."**

- The teacher was at Bolton Elementary School in Cleveland, c. 1922.
- Source: Owens, *Jesse: The Man Who Outran Hitler* (1978); recounted
  in Baker (1986).

**Charles Riley** — first track coach at Fairmount Junior High in
Cleveland; trained Owens at 6 AM because Owens worked after school.

- Primary: Owens consistently credited Riley in his speaking
  engagements throughout his life and in *Blackthink* (1970).
- Secondary: Baker (1986), ch. 2; Schaap, *Triumph* (2007), pp. 18–21.
- Riley was Irish-American, white, and remained a lifelong friend of Owens.

**Minnie Ruth Solomon** — Owens' wife; met at Fairmount Junior High
when he was 15 and she was 13; first daughter Gloria born 1932;
married July 5, 1935.

- Primary: Owens family records; Owens' own writings.
- Secondary: Baker (1986), ch. 3; Schaap (2007), ch. 2. Two later
  daughters: Marlene (b. 1937) and Beverly (b. 1940).
- Ruth Owens (1915–2001) outlived her husband by 21 years.

**Ohio State University: no athletic scholarship; worked jobs to pay
tuition; couldn't live on campus; "colored only" restaurants when traveling.**

- Source: Baker (1986), ch. 3 — specifically on segregation policies
  affecting Owens at OSU. Also documented in Schaap (2007).

**Coach Larry Snyder; "Buckeye Bullet" nickname.**

- Source: Larry Snyder coached Owens at OSU 1934–1936. Documented in
  OSU athletic records (Ohio State University Archives) and Baker (1986).

**"Day of Days" — Big Ten Championships, Ferry Field, Ann Arbor,
May 25, 1935; tied one world record and set five more in 45 minutes.**

- Primary: Big Ten Conference official records (Big Ten Conference
  archives, Park Ridge, Illinois).
- Secondary: Richard C. Crepeau, "Owens at the Big Ten" in
  *Journal of Sport History*; Baker (1986), ch. 4; Schaap (2007), ch. 3.
- The records set or tied: 100-yard dash (9.4 sec, tied), long jump
  (8.13 m world record that stood for 25 years), 220-yard sprint, 220-yard
  hurdles (the first time broken under 23 sec), and the metric equivalents
  of the 220-yard records (counted as additional records for 200m flat
  and 200m hurdles).

**"First time he had ever left the United States" — sailed on the SS
Manhattan in July 1936.**

- Source: 1936 US Olympic Committee official report; Schaap (2007),
  ch. 5 on the team's departure. Owens had never traveled outside the
  US prior to the 1936 Olympics.

### Panel II additions

**18 African-American athletes on the 1936 US Olympic team.**

- Source: 1936 US Olympic Committee official roster. The names
  mentioned (Ralph Metcalfe, Cornelius Johnson, Archie Williams,
  Mack Robinson) are documented in the official team records.
- Schaap (2007) discusses the diversity of the team.

**Marty Glickman and Sam Stoller** — the team's only two Jewish

- Primary: Marty Glickman, *The Fastest Kid on the Block: The Marty

- Secondary: Schaap (2007), ch. 18; Baker (1986).
- The decision was attributed to Avery Brundage and head coach
  Lawson Robertson. Glickman went on to a celebrated career as a
  sports broadcaster (Knicks, Giants, Jets) and spoke publicly about
  this incident for the rest of his life.
- Documentary: *Glickman* (HBO, 2013, dir. James L. Freedman).

**NAACP's argument — that African-Americans should refuse to lend
legitimacy to a racist regime.**

- Primary: NAACP correspondence files, Library of Congress (Manuscript

- Walter White's unsent letter to Owens (December 1935) is preserved

- Secondary: Mandell, *The Nazi Olympics* (1971), ch. 3; Baker (1986).

**Helene Mayer** — Jewish German fencer invited back from America for

- Primary: Olympic Games records, Berlin 1936.
- Secondary: Milly Mogulof, *Foiled: Hitler's Jewish Olympian — The

- Bachrach, *The Nazi Olympics* (2000); Mandell (1971).
- Mayer was the only person of Jewish descent the Nazi regime
  permitted to compete for Germany. Her case was used by the regime
  to deflect international boycott pressure.

**Adi Dassler shoes — first sponsorship of a Black male athlete in
history; Dassler was a Nazi Party member.**

- Primary: Gebrüder Dassler Schuhfabrik company records (held in the

- Secondary: Barbara Smit, *Sneaker Wars: The Enemy Brothers Who
  Founded Adidas and Puma and the Family Feud That Forever Changed
  the Business of Sports* (Ecco/HarperCollins, 2008) — the definitive
  history of the Dassler brothers and their relationship to the
  Nazi regime.
- Adi Dassler joined the NSDAP in May 1933 (German Federal Archives,
  NSDAP membership records).
- Schaap (2007) discusses the Dassler/Owens shoe incident.

**Handshake-myth origin — created by American sportswriters in the
days after the 100m race.**

- The exact first source of the "handshake snub" story has been
  traced to American newspaper coverage in early August 1936.
- Secondary: David Clay Large, *Nazi Games: The Olympics of 1936*
  (W. W. Norton, 2007), ch. 13, contains a detailed treatment of
  how the myth originated and spread. Baker (1986) discusses Owens'
  efforts to correct it throughout his life.
- Owens debunked the story in his own writings (*Blackthink*, 1970)
  and in many interviews, but the myth persisted.

## PHOTOGRAPHS AND VISUAL MATERIALS

### The iconic Owens + Long Berlin 1936 photographs

The nine photographs Dr. B uploaded showing Owens and Long together —
lying on the long-jump pit, conversing on the field, embracing —
are part of a widely distributed body of images taken at the
Reichssportfeld on **August 4, 1936** (the day of the long-jump
final). Multiple photographers and press agencies captured these
moments; the same iconic poses appear in archives across the world
because both the German press corps and Leni Riefenstahl's
*Olympia*-Film unit were present.

### Known photographers and source agencies

- **Arthur Grimm** — German photographer attached to Leni Riefenstahl's
  *Olympia*-Film production unit. The "Olympia-Film" stamp on the
  reverse of original prints credits Grimm specifically. Grimm shot
  action images of Long mid-jump on August 4, 1936. *(Verified at
  auction provenance — SCP Auctions, Luz Long Collection.)*

- **Scherl** (Berlin) — major German press agency operating in 1936.
  Iconic images of Owens and Long in conversation are credited to
  Scherl in modern rights-house databases. Scherl's negatives are
  largely held today by **Süddeutsche Zeitung Photo** (Munich) and
  licensed via **Bridgeman Images** and **Imago Images**.

- **Presse-Bild-Zentrale Braemer & Güll** (Berlin) — German photo
  publisher that produced a 12-card set, *Berlin 1936 Olympische
  Spiele*, which includes the famous "Owens and Long on the lawn"
  pose. Originals from this series are now held in private
  collections and various archives.

- **Bundesarchiv** (German Federal Archives, Berlin/Koblenz) —
  holds many Owens images in the Bild 183 series. Some have been
  released under CC-BY-SA 3.0 DE; others remain rights-restricted.

- **Heinrich Hoffmann archive** — Hitler's personal photographer
  also documented the Games; some images of Owens originate here.

### Modern archives and licensing

- **Bridgeman Images** — Scherl collection, including the specific
  Owens + Long photographs. License required for commercial use.
  https://www.bridgemanimages.com
- **Getty Images** — extensive Berlin 1936 collection including
  Owens + Long images. License required.
  https://www.gettyimages.com
- **Imago Images** — German rights house holding Scherl-era prints.

- **Bundesarchiv** — some images CC-BY-SA 3.0 DE (free to use with
  attribution); search their catalogue:
  https://www.bild.bundesarchiv.de
