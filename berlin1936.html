Berlin 1936

Turn sound up · ~3 minutes
August 3, 1936
BERLIN
One hundred ten thousand people.
Forty-nine nations.
The largest stadium ever built for an Olympic Games.
A twenty-two-year-old from Oakville, Alabama is about to make the host country look very small in front of all of them. His name is James Cleveland Owens. Everyone calls him Jesse.
Event Three  ·  The Hundred Metres
3:15 P.M.
10.3
seconds  ·  gold
The 100. The 200. The long jump. The 4×100. Four gold medals in eight days. A record that holds for forty-eight years.
The Medal Ceremony
He raises his hand. The American anthem plays. Somewhere in a private box, a small man does not stand.
A German athlete named Luz Long — the man who finished second in the long jump — walks across the field and embraces him. They will write to each other until Long dies on the Italian front in 1943.
"Hitler didn't snub me.
It was our President who snubbed me." — Jesse Owens
He came home with four gold medals. And entered his own reception at the Waldorf Astoria through the back door.
Ninety Years Later
A Track in Brooklyn.
Four people are standing near the finish line at golden hour. Two are us. Two are runners catching their breath.
Darius
"Owens' story is wild. Hitler refused to shake his hand. Walked out of the stadium."
Dr. Niia Bishop
"Actually — Hitler acknowledged every winner the day before. The IOC told him: all of them or none. He chose none. It wasn't personal to Jesse."
Marisol
"Wait. So the Nazi wasn't the one who snubbed him?"
Adam Slocum
"Roosevelt never sent a telegram. Never called. Owens entered his own reception at the Waldorf Astoria through the back door."
Darius
"The American president."
Dr. Niia Bishop
"It took thirty-nine years before a sitting president put a medal on his chest. Gerald Ford. 1976."
The handshake snub never happened. It was invented by American sportswriters who needed a villain abroad so they wouldn't have to write about the one at home.
Marisol
"That's the story nobody tells."
Dr. Niia Bishop
"That's exactly why we're building the museum."
