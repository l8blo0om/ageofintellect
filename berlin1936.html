Panel I  ·  Cinematic

Berlin, August 1936.

One hundred ten thousand people. Forty-nine nations. The largest stadium ever built for an Olympic Games. And a twenty-two-year-old from Oakville, Alabama who is about to make the host country look very small in front of all of them.

His name was James Cleveland Owens. Everyone called him Jesse.

Panel II  ·  Commentary

Two witnesses at the event.

Dr. Niia Bishop and Adam Slocum — on location in period dress — set up the story most people think they know, and the one almost no one does.

"Most people remember this day for one thing. They remember it wrong."

Panel III  ·  Immersive

The hundred metres.

Row eleven, west grandstand. A man two seats down holds a pocket watch open in his palm. He is timing it himself — he does not trust the officials. The starter raises the pistol.

Ten point three seconds. Gold. — the first of four in eight days. A record that holds for forty-eight years.

Panel IV  ·  The Real Story

Ninety years later. A track in Brooklyn.

Two young runners — Darius and Marisol — carry the version of the story everyone is taught: that Hitler refused to shake Jesse's hand. The version that lets the country off the hook. Niia and Adam set the record straight.

"Hitler didn't snub me. It was our President who snubbed me."
— Jesse Owens
