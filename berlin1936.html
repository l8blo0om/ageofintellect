Panel I  ·  Cinematic

Berlin, August 1936.

Adolf Hitler had paused his plans for a coronation ceremony to welcome the entire world to his nation for the 11th Olympic Games. Germans of all classes and over 150,000 foreigners of means were watching in person, another 162,000 urban Germans in Berlin and Potsdam watched via live broadcast, and people from countless nations were tuned in to their radios. Hitler's book Mein Kampf had called it "a sin against all reason to train a born half-ape." A twenty-two-year-old from Oakville, Alabama was set to prove to everyone with eyes to see and ears to hear that he was not half-ape, nor three-fifths of a man. He was all man — and with the Lord's strength he just might become Superman over the next eight days.

Hitler had spent $25 million turning Berlin into a stage set. Anti-Semitic posters were taken down. Buildings were whitewashed. The city was rehearsed, polished, and held its pose. The Reichssportfeld, the new Olympic stadium complex built into a Berlin hillside, was the centerpiece. Granite, limestone, Olympic rings cast in bronze. The Games were meant as a coronation of Aryan supremacy — the Nazi belief in white European racial superiority — in front of the entire world.

James Cleveland Owens — son of a sharecropper, grandson of a slave — was born in Oakville, Alabama. He picked cotton as a boy. When he was nine, his family moved north to Cleveland, Ohio, part of the Great Migration: millions of African-Americans leaving the segregated South for the industrial North in search of a future their country wouldn't give them. A Cleveland schoolteacher misheard his Alabama-accented "J.C." as "Jesse." The name stuck.

His first coach was Charles Riley, the white track coach at Fairmount Junior High in Cleveland. Riley spotted Owens' speed at age twelve and noticed he couldn't make team practice — Owens worked an after-school job to help his family. So Riley got up at six in the morning every weekday and trained him in the empty schoolyard, alone, for years. Owens said his career started in that morning sun.

He met Minnie Ruth Solomon at Fairmount Junior High when he was fifteen and she was thirteen. They dated through high school and had their first daughter, Gloria, in 1932. They married on July 5, 1935 — one year, almost exactly, before Berlin. They would have two more daughters together, Marlene and Beverly. Ruth stayed with him until his death.

At Ohio State University he received no athletic scholarship. He worked part-time jobs to pay tuition, couldn't live on campus, and ate at "colored only" restaurants when the team traveled. His coach, Larry Snyder, turned him into "the Buckeye Bullet." On a single afternoon in May 1935, at the Big Ten Championships in Ann Arbor, Michigan, he tied one world record and set five others in forty-five minutes. Sportswriters called it the greatest forty-five minutes in the history of sport.

He was twenty-two when he boarded the SS Manhattan in New York Harbor in July 1936. It was the first time he had ever left the United States. He had won eight individual NCAA championships — more than anyone in college track history, before or since.

In many parts of his own country, he could not be born in the same hospital, swear on the same Bible, or be buried in the same ground as the neighbors he lived among. He had come to run anyway. He had come to represent his country, no matter how he felt about that country … and no matter how that country felt about him.

Panel II  ·  Commentary

Back to August 1936.

Dr. Niia Bishop and Adam Slocum take the viewer back in time and space — to the Reichssportfeld stands, to the eight days that broke a regime's myth, and a country's promise.

The American team almost didn't go to Berlin at all. The Nuremberg Laws — the Nazi statutes that stripped German Jews of citizenship and outlawed marriages between Jews and non-Jews — had been on the books for nearly a year. Jewish athletes had been systematically barred from training facilities, sports clubs, even from competing for their own country. The NAACP and several American Jewish organizations urged a boycott. The NAACP's argument was sharp: African-Americans, of all people, knew what state-sponsored racial supremacy looked like — and should refuse to lend it the legitimacy of their athletic excellence. NAACP Secretary Walter Francis White drafted a letter urging Owens not to go. (He never sent it.) Owens himself, briefly, agreed. "If there are minorities in Germany who are being discriminated against," he said publicly, "the United States should withdraw from the 1936 Olympics."

Avery Brundage, head of the American Olympic Committee, was unmoved. He sailed to Germany to look into the rumors himself, was hosted by Nazi officials, and reported back that the persecution had been exaggerated. He called the boycott supporters "un-American agitators." The team set sail.

East gate of the Olympic Stadium, August 1, 1936  ·  Library of Congress, Prints and Photographs Division  ·  Public domain

The team that sailed was the most racially diverse the Olympics had ever seen — eighteen African-American athletes, by far the most before or for many years after. Owens was one of them, alongside sprinter Ralph Metcalfe (his college friend, who would finish a tenth of a second behind him in the 100), high jumper Cornelius Johnson, 400-metre runner Archie Williams, and Mack Robinson — older brother of a kid named Jackie.

Two American sprinters never got to race. Marty Glickman and Sam Stoller — the team's only two Jewish athletes — were pulled from the 4×100 relay the morning of the final and replaced with Owens and Metcalfe. The decision was attributed to Brundage and head coach Lawson Robertson. Glickman, who would go on to become one of America's most famous sports broadcasters, spent the rest of his life calling it what it was.

On the German side, Helene Mayer — a Jewish fencer who had been living in America for two years — was invited back to compete for Germany so the regime could deflect international criticism. She was the only Jewish athlete the Nazis permitted on their team. She won silver. On the medal podium, she gave the Nazi salute. The story everyone now tells is Owens; Helene Mayer is the story the regime curated to look acceptable.

And one quiet thing happened just before the races. Adolf "Adi" Dassler, the German shoemaker who would later found Adidas, walked into the Olympic Village uninvited with a pair of hand-made track spikes. He gave them to Owens. Owens wore them. It was the first sponsorship of a Black male athlete in history. Dassler was a Nazi Party member. The contradictions never landed on any single person — they all landed at once on Owens.

The story everyone has been told is simple: Hitler refused to shake Jesse Owens' hand. He walked out of the stadium. He could not bear to acknowledge an African-American man's victory. This story is not true. Not the way it's been told. What actually happened is more complicated, and ultimately more revealing — about a different country.

The myth was created by American sportswriters in the days right after the 100m race, hunting for a clean villain narrative. By the time Owens sailed home, the story was already running on three continents. He spent the rest of his life politely correcting it. Most people kept telling it anyway.

Panel III  ·  Immersive

The hundred metres.

Row eleven, west grandstand. A man two seats down holds a pocket watch open in his palm. He is timing it himself — he does not trust the officials. The starter raises the pistol. The stadium folds itself into a single, low hum.

Four gold medals in eight days. A record that holds for forty-eight years, until Carl Lewis matches it in Los Angeles in 1984. The crowd — one hundred thousand Germans, told for years what to believe about the people Owens came from — chanted his name. "Yes-say O-vens. Yes-say O-vens."

"When I came back to my native country, after all the stories about Hitler, I couldn't ride in the front of the bus. I had to go to the back door. I couldn't live where I wanted." — Jesse Owens

And in the middle of the long-jump competition — when Owens had fouled his first two attempts and was one bad jump from elimination — a German athlete named Luz Long walked over and suggested he take off from six inches behind the board. Owens qualified. Long finished second. Owens won gold. Long crossed the field, embraced him in full view of Hitler's box, and walked him on a victory lap arm in arm. They wrote to each other for seven years. Ludwig "Luz" Long — conscripted into the Wehrmacht, the German army — was killed in battle on Sicily during the Allied invasion on July 14, 1943. He was thirty.

Long had written Owens one last letter from Sicily. If he didn't come home, he asked Owens to find his son Kai after the war. The letter was delivered after Long's death. Owens kept it for the rest of his life. Decades later, he tracked Kai Long down in West Germany and stood with him at his father's grave.

Panel IV  ·  The Real Story

Ninety years later. A track in Brooklyn.

Two young runners — Darius and Marisol — carry the version of the story everyone is taught: that Hitler refused to shake Jesse's hand. The version that lets the country off the hook. Dr. Bishop sets the record straight.

"Hitler didn't snub me. It was our President who snubbed me. The President didn't even send me a telegram." — Jesse Owens

Franklin Roosevelt never called. Never wrote. Never invited Owens to the White House. When Owens returned to a ticker-tape parade in New York, he and his family could not find a hotel in Manhattan that would take them. He entered his own reception at the Waldorf Astoria through the service entrance — because the front door was off-limits to African-American guests. The four-time gold medalist took the freight elevator to his own banquet.

A sharecropper's son from Oakville, Alabama wins his first gold because his German competitor helps him. They embrace in front of Hitler's box. They write each other for seven years, until a war ends it. Owens comes home and cannot enter his own banquet through the front door.

The country that was supposed to hate him gave him a friend. The country that was supposed to love him gave him the service elevator. That's the irony — and the beauty.

Sport doesn't fix the world. It shows us what is there. And what is there is always more than one thing at once.

Jesse Owens

Before Martin Luther King, Jr. had a dream, James Cleveland Owens was living his.

He wasn't in Germany to show off or show out.

He was there to find out for himself what happened when he did the very best he could, for as long as he could. One step at a time. One day at a time. One race at a time. Medal after medal, he found that his true competitor was his former best.

In 10.3 seconds, he didn't just break a record. He challenged a worldview that insisted he was inferior.

His talent was undeniable. His spirit was indomitable. His courage was fierce.

Ultimately, Jesse Owens represents something larger than any nation, race, or era.

He is an icon of what is possible if we unwaveringly believe in ourselves, no matter what anyone says or thinks about us.
